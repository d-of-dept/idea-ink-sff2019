Yee Hui Chia

If bad drivers can drive, why can’t bad artists draw?

“

For every person that says “I can’t draw to save my life,” or that “You’re so talented!”, when they see a person drawing with confidence – I can, with full authority, tell you two things.

One: Drawing, like writing, and arithmetic, is a skill that can be taught – and hence, learnt.

Two: Like your first gym session, or cycling attempt, drawing is a psychomotor skill – which requires practice, and can be built gradually, over time.

Forget what you learnt in art classes at school: that art is a vaunted privilege for the talented few, belonging within the white-washed gallows of modern art galleries.

What we are dealing with today, is functional image-making. From your profile pictures on LinkedIn, to every PowerPoint slide deck, PDF file, and Outlook e-mail that passes through your fingertips, we are dealing with a world of pixels – where your medium may have already drowned out your message.

For every Millennial and Gen-Z your company is trying to woo, for every C-suite leader you’re trying to capture precious minutes from – even your own teammates, who might be crossing several time zones and events during a concurrent video call – we are living in an increasingly information-rich, yet attention-poor workplace. 

So why do good visuals matter?

“

Like a good piece of design, good visuals are elegant, compact, useful, and above all else, memorable.

Good design doesn’t shout; it communicates. The visuals that you use every day – be it an emoji, flowchart, or video – are expressions that will either reinforce, or detract from the message you’re trying to convey.

Unfortunately, whether you like it or not, these visual messages do represent your ideas – your thought process – and sometimes, even your work image.

The ability to communicate memorably is a skill that requires discipline, craft, and constant effort. It is something that will augment and strengthen good ideas, and powerful strategies.

While we are taught not to judge a book by its cover, there are indeed times where we only get about five seconds to issue one key message, to draw a crowded room’s attention, or to impose upon the urgency of a particular context, challenge, or solution. 

At Idea Ink, we are trained precisely to rise to this occasion; to parse and synthesize complexity into succinct visual images.
